Miracle in Venezuela?

Date: 10-12-2017

Source: Macroeconomics Class; BS I exposed to

Summary

The South American nation of Venezuela has had one of the world’s fastest-growing nominal GDPs since 1997. Between 1997 and 2007, Venezuelan nominal GDP grew by an average of 28% each year. The growth rate is much faster than nominal GDP in the United States or even in booming economies like China. So, Venezuela must be experiencing an economic miracle.

Argument

        In order to determine if Venezuela's economics is growing at a such fast speed, we need to first figure out what "normal GDP" means and how it is different from real GDP. Nominal GDP is measured using today's aggregate price level times the output in any given year. Real GDP is measured by calculating price times output quantity, assuming prices somehow stayed “constant” at a base year’s prices. In short, real GDP uses constant prices when calculating GDP so as to eliminate the inflation factor, while "norminal GDP" does not factor out price inflation. See the graph(from Econ 201 slide) on the left, Venezuela did not experience a fast economic growth, but suffered from a unusually high inflation.
        This claim should be doubted because it seems too good to be true. It falls into "tell partial story" and "mutate the fact" categories because the producer did not mention about "Real GDP" and mutated the fact of Venezuela's economics growth. However, whether the producer deliberately hid the other part of the story or was just ignorant of the truth is hard to tell. Thus, this claim does not meet "lie about one's belief" or "careless about the truth".

Conclusion

        The intention of this claim is unclear due to unknown context of the claim producer. This claim is to the public and meets two criteria, so the magnitude is 3.
